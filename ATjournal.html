ALAN BECK’s


APPALACHIAN TRAIL


JOURNAL

















Preface



I had never really seriously thought about walking the entire length of the Appalachian Trail growing up, though I had grown up hiking in New Hampshire since the mid 1970s.


My sister Karen and I on top of Monadnock, 1979


I was introduced to hiking at the age of four by my parents on Mount Monadnock of NH (3162'), because the mountain was a few miles from my aunt's cottage. My family and I spent two weeks in Harrisville, NH every summer. Every year, my mother, father, sister and I climbed the mountain as an annual trip once in our 2 week stay at the cottage. By the time I was 9 years old, I was growing tired of the easier trail on the mountain, the White Cross / White Dot trail, and had to try the more difficult 9 mile roundtrip hike on the Pumpelly Trail.























My first hike on the AT up Carter Dome, 1979


At the same time that I felt that I needed to do the harder hike up Mount Monadnock, my father decided to have the two of us try an AMC guided White Mountain hike. My first AMC guided hike happened when I was nine years old. One day of the guided hike went from the hut along the AT up Carter Dome, and back to the hut for a loop hike of 15 miles. For a 9 year old and his father, who were both beginner hikers at the time, this was quite an experience.


It was more interesting because on the way to get to the starting point of our hike, our car ended up rolling down a hill of the median of I-93 due to our half flat tire. This was caused by, in part, a young nine year old boy wanting to lie down in the back seat and take a nap. Due to that snafu, we arrived a half hour late for the beginning of the hike. The rest of the group started without us, but one leader, Misha Kirk, was waiting for us. Due to my age, my father and I were advised by the guide not to hike out (on the AT) over Wildcat Mountain as the rest of the group did.


We enjoyed our hike to Carter Dome and our first taste of NH 4000'ers. My father and I had to keep doing the higher mountains to challenge ourselves. We wanted to do Mount Washington the next year (1982), only to be turned back by bad weather on another AMC guided hike trip. Our group climbed a hard Madison Gulf Trail from Pinkham Notch to Madison Springs Hut. The potential weather on the northern Presidential ridge was too risky the next day to risk the hiking over to Lakes of the Clouds Hut (on the AT). Our group got bussed around to the Lakes of the Clouds Hut access trail (the Ammonossuc Ravine Trail), but my father did not want to hike, so I had to go back to Pinkham Notch with him. I was not a happy 11 year old!


The next year when I was 12, the two of us climbed up Tuckermans Ravine on Mount Washington solo. That hike was where I first felt the pain of blisters, because I had a brand new pair of boots. All that I really remember was I went through an entire package of moleskin. We arrived back at Pinkham Notch camp so late that we had to practically beg for our supper. They did have some extra soup for two exhausted hikers that came back at 8pm.


My cousin, Drew, and I on Lafayette Ridge, 1982


Our other annual family vacation spot was Lafayette Campground in Franconia Notch. When I was in my pre teens, our family would do a short hike up to the AT at Lonesome Lake Hut to play around the lake. As I got older, that trip turned into riding up the Cannon Tramway and hiking back to the campground from the summit of Cannon. In 1983, while I was vacationing at Lafayette Campground, I would hike Mount Lafayette (the second highest mountain in the White Mountains, on the AT) as a 12 year old with my father, my uncle and my 13 year old cousin Drew.


The remaining years of grade school, my father and I finished most of the rest of our NH 4000'ers by doing various day hikes from our summer family campgrounds. We combine this with going other AMC guided hut to hut trips. This started to introduce me to the entire White Mountain region.


An annual family camping week at Lafayette Campground in the eastern White Mountains turned into a week at Jigger Johnson Campground in the southern White Mountains for five years. This let me hike more southern NH 4000ers with my father such as Passaconway and the Tripyramids. My mother and sister joined us for the Tripyramid hike. We all hiked the first peak of North Tripyramid together and my mother and sister waited as my father and climbed the second peak of Middle Tripyramid. My sister slipped and fell on a rock on the descent and stopped hiking for the rest of our family camping vacations.


The family decided to move our campsite to Sugarloaf Campground in the northern White Mountains. I was a teenage peakbagger (hiking mountains on a list) at the time, it let me have greater access to all the 4000 foot mountains of the White Mountains.


My interest in the Appalachian Trail really broadened in 1985, when I went on a family vacation which involved driving on Skyline Drive, We stopped along the AT and hiked small sections of it that crossed the highway.


My father and I had made very good progress on our four thousand footers by the time I was in high school. We decided to try backpacking trips to knock off a few mountains off of our NH48 lists at once. Our overnight trips to this point were overnight at AMC huts.


My first backpacking trip was in the summer of my last year of middle school, 1985, along the Carter Ridge. My father and I hiked to Mount Moriah from the town of Gorham. We had superb views of Gorham from the ridges of Mount Moriah and hiked along the Carter Moriah Trail (also the Appalachian Trail). We overnighted at Imp Shelter before proceeding to Carter Notch Hut. We hiked the next day to Carter Notch Hut and back to having meals prepared for us and to having blankets provided for us at the AMC huts.


We hiked over Wildcat Mountain to AMC's Pinkham Notch Camp. I would later find out that this was one of the harder portions of the entire Appalachian Trail. This trip seemed strange as I had my Boy Scout equipment, a headlamp that looked like what a coal miner would wear, wool socks and a giant salisbury steak dehydrated meal that I cooked on my heavy French Camping Gaz stove. I did get a new internal frame pack for this hike with a giant Sears sleeping bag lashed to the bottom portion of the pack.


my father and I at Imp shelter, 1985


My second backpacking trip in 1988 was an interesting one. My father and I planned to knock off one of the most remote peaks of the NH 4000'ers, Owls Head, along with the Bonds, hiking another portion of the AT. This trip was another combination backpack/day hike since the only camping overnight that we did was at Franconia Brook Campsite, which was only 3 miles in from the road via a trail that was on an old railroad bed, called the Wilderness Trail.


This was the first trip that I experienced much more than I bargained for. My father and I climbed through mud up the unmarked trail to Owls Head. We hiked down to the 13 falls campsite by 6pm. However, we were supposed to be at the Galehead Hut 3 miles uphill by dinnertime. My father sent me ahead and I ran up to the hut. The hut croo organized a small search and rescue crew to look for him. By the time the crew had gotten organized to go out, he was almost at the hut, a little tired, but not super exhausted. He sat down in the middle of the trail for a bit to rest.


The next hiking day was a long day on part of the AT over the Bonds. We had to climb down Bondcliff and hike the 5 miles out to Lincoln Woods. We got to the bottom of Bondcliff to an old hikers campsite, Camp 16, at 6pm. It was only a six mile flat walk out the Wilderness Trail on a railroad bed. We both were feeling a bit tired from the day before, so my father sent me ahead to hurry back to the car. He ended up crossing the river (at the site of the Franconia Brook campground) three miles from the road to walk back to the car along a dirt road that rangers went up sometimes. My father was waiting for me as I got out of the woods as complete darkness descended. He gotten a ride back to the car from a ranger who was driving on the dirt road that my father had crossed the river to get to.


























First glimpse of Mount Katahdin on family vacation, 1988


The summer before I started my senior year of high school in 1988, I was finally able to stop by Mount Katahdin on a family trip coming back from Quebec. I was so excited that I could finally see the end of the Appalachian Trail that I had only read about in the past. My father and I hiked for ten minutes up the Hunt trail from Katahdin Stream Campground. I wanted to hike further, but we had to get home after a fairly long vacation.


My third backpacking trip in 1991 had a goal of knocking off three White Mountain peaks that were not easily accessible from one point. This was in a region called the Pemi Wilderness. This involved hiking over two 4000'ers, the Hancocks, on the way in and one, Carragain, on the way out. The first day went as expected as we climbed the Hancocks and camped at Desolation shelter. This shelter was further along the Wilderness Trail than we had been in the previous year. The second day turned into another day of longer mileage than we had planned on. Our hike involved hiking up and over Mt. Carrigain and down to Sawyer Pond. We got to this trailhead at 6pm and would have been a normal starting point for day hikers. We still had quite a distance to walk to the end of that dirt road and another 5 miles to hike to get to the main road, the Kancamaugus Highway.

We got to the main road at 11pm and had another 5 miles of an uphill road walk to get back to our car. Like the previous year, I was sent ahead to walk back to the car while my father stayed at the side of the road. The results were the same, where I walked back to the Hancock Pass trailhead along the road and my father got there at the same time by getting a ride from a ranger. On the drive home in Lincoln, still in the White Mountains, we got pulled over for having a taillight out. Thank goodness it was only a warning, because we were not in the best of moods at 1am when we got that warning!


I had three years of college before I finished the 4000'ers in 1992 at the age of 21 by climbing Osceola and East Osceola with my father. The accomplishment of hiking all 48 of the NH footers did not set in until much later, but it did teach me that although hiking goals might seem unrealistic, they are achievable with time. I did all 48 NH 4000 footers with my father making it quite an achievement for both of us.


My next hiking goals which seemed unreachable were the New England 4000'ers, a White Mountain hut traverse and a Presidential traverse. These were all eventually finished, but I would finish them all during or soon after I had thru hiked the Appalachian Trail.


I led various friends on day hikes throughout the White Mountain area during much of my twenties. I was not hiking as much as I was settling into my first professional job.


I started to hike regularly again once I got active in the Boston Chapter of the Appalachian Mountain Club. However, combined with the rigors of working my first professional job, I did not have time to do anything more than dayhike.


Layoffs started to happen at my old company in 2000, I left my apartment near Foxboro, Mass. and put my stuff in a storage locker. The stuff would remain in that locker until after my thruhike. Being out of work for nearly a year in 2001, I had lots of time to hike most of the state parks of Massachusetts and generally explore the state with my retired father.
























First winter hike, Mount Washington, 2001


I was out of professional work for a year in the spring of 2001. I decided to work again at a place I would remain for nearly two years, Eastern Mountain Sports. I had regularly shopped at EMS in the past. This job gave me the knowledge base that I would need to outfit other hikers as well as myself for longer and more difficult trips. This job also got me to hike more often since all of the stuff was right beside me as I worked. It was at this point, that I started hiking more and more and I started to hike year round. I started winter hiking by going with two EMS coworkers up Mount Washington in November 2001 with two feet of snow on the ground and a -20 degree windchill. That helped me prepare for potential terrible weather conditions in the future.


I worked at EMS full time for one year before I decided to take a college certificate program over the next three semesters. I said to myself, if I got that certificate and still had no opportunity to return to my career, I would start my thruhike. The winter semester of 2002 / 2003 came and the computer job market did not look very good, I started training in earnest for a thru hike by backpacking a 70 mile section of the Maine Appalachian Trail.












Brian, Cranbo and I on top of Bigelow, first day of warmup hike



Warmup


My longtime hiking friend and fellow EMS coworker Brian and I arrived in Portland, Maine, to my college friend Cranbo's apartment at 9pm to get an early start on our four hour hike the next day. Two other old college friends of mine, Barry and Mike, stopped by for the night. Instead of getting a good night’s rest for the big trip the next day, the four of us (not Brian, he was packing) went to the the nightclubs of Portland.


We finally got driving to our hiking destinations after 10am. Brian, Cranbo and I had three separate cars to spot, so we all had to drive to Monson initially. We left my car there and took a crazy road from the Monson area (Shirley) over to the Caratunk area (Moxie Pond) where we were going to spot Brian’s truck to have a resupply point halfway along the route. This dirt road had sections that looked as if they hadn’t been maintained in 30 years. There were drains that used to run under the road, that were exposed. Without Cranbo’s jeep and Brian’s truck, we wouldn’t have been able to drive that route. We left Brian’s truck in Caratunk at an AT parking lot by the Kennebec River. The three of us piled into Cranbo’s jeep. We had almost no room since we had three full backpacks with us. We did manage to squeeze everything in, after taking the top off of Cranbo's jeep.


We got to Bigelow trailhead at 5:30pm after spotting all cars. We had 4 hours of hiking to get to the Avery Shelter below the summit of Mount Bigelow. It got dark about halfway thru the hike, but the moon was so bright, that I barely needed a headlamp until the last few minutes of the hike. We got to the Avery tentsite, where we expected a shelter, but there were only tentsites at 9:30pm. We probably didn’t settle in until at least midnight. This would be my first night of animal viewing since I saw a jackrabbit staring right at me from the outhouse at the campsite. This is where I learned that pine needles floating in the water of your soup are not the most appetizing thing to a hungry backpacker. The water sources were so dry that year that water was hard to find along the trail.


We woke up early the next morning and filled our water bottles with the scant water at the tentsite. We ate our breakfast bars and hiked straight to the top of Bigelow, Avery Peak. It was a great day where we could see views in all directions from the bare summit. It was noon when we got to the summit, so Brian and I said our farewells to Cranbo and headed off to the Little Bigelow shelter.


Brian and I leaving Cranbo to start backpack


We climbed the three peaks of Bigelow and descended to get to the Little Bigelow shelter. This shelter is where we started to meet our northbound thru hiker friends. We met a thru hiker named TurkeyBacon tonight, a southern Illinois guy who got his trailname by bringing a Turkey bacon sub with him on the first night of his AT hike instead of cooking. Cranbo ended up running into this guy on his trip down the AT off Bigelow on his second day of hiking and called this person an amusing ‘beardy-weirdy’.


This shelter is also where we heard about El Guapo, an interesting person who would be a day ahead of us for our whole trip. We would always read about him in shelter journals daily. We also had a couple of guys at this shelter who served the entire summer on the Baxter State Park trailcrew. This shelter was interesting since it had a number of 6 foot deep swimming holes (the Holes) formed by the stream near it. These were pools of water that Brian went into, but I did not since the water was ice cold! We heard and saw the reflection of lightning all night long, but did not get rained on. Brian and I figured that the mountain ranges to the south (Sugarloaf, Bigelow) were blocking this thunderstorm out.


We had already finished the mountainous part of our trip by hiking down from the Bigelow Ridge. As proud as we were of this, Brian and I stopped at the end of Flagstaff Lake and admired the view of Little Bigelow and a good portion of the 30 mile long lake that is on one side of the entire Bigelow Range. We continued past the 2000 mile marker of the Appalachian Trail (painted in the middle of the street) on Long Falls Dam Road. We hiked up another tiny hill to get to the West Carry Pond shelter. We stopped here for a short lunch, and then headed on to a small beach on the shore of East Carry pond to pitch our tents to camp out.


The tentsite we chose was interesting because we could go swimming in the 3 foot deep lake and rinse out our clothes. We had lots of fun watching all the small leeches in the lake be attracted to various bits of food, etc.


We had a thru hiker named Papa Geezer from Rangeley, Maine, stop by a couple hours after we made camp and pitch his tent in the clearing that we found. He was a thru hiker that had just hopped back on the trail after a hiatus spent at home in Rangeley. My guess is that he was in his 60’s.


We woke up on the shores of the East Carry Pond to discover that it was raining. I decided I did not want to get out of my tent until the rain stopped. The rain stopped at 6:30 and I decided to crawl out of my tent, only to find that Papa Geezer had already left. We got going at 8am and made it to the Pierce Pond Shelter for an extended lunch. We had to wait until 3pm at the Kennebec River to catch the Kennebec Ferry.


We had lunch at the Pierce Pond shelter, (easily the nicest location for a shelter that we passed on the trail) and met yet another interesting person. This was a girl with a trail name of Tadpole, who was out of work, and wandering between shelters on the AT in Maine. She was a little strange wearing a wiccan necklace and had a marijuana leaf tattoo on her chest. It was also interesting reading in the shelter journal what our fellow thru hikers, TurkeyBacon and Papa Geezer were up to. TurkeyBacon had an amusing story in the shelter’s journal about a frog that jumped at him on the trail. (The frogs were everywhere throughout this trip!)


We met a Rhode Island URI student, Phil, who ended up hiking down to the Kennebec River with us. He told us how he just had spent his 21st

birthday on the trail and his fellow thru hikers decided to help him celebrate his birthday in a town along the trail.


The trail down to the Kennebec River was one of the most scenic of our entire hike, taking us by many pretty waterfalls right along a mountain stream. There was a log bridge on the trail going down towards the river without a railing which I almost fell off of!


We got to the 3pm Kennebec River ferry at 3:30pm since the trail down was steeper and rougher than I had planned. The Maine ATC hired a local outfitter, Rivers and Trails, to offer canoe service across the Kennebec for hikers twice a day. We got to the (mighty, as we called it) Kennebec, Brian and I were in the ATC canoe and our friend, Phil, ended up getting a ride across from some friendly canoeists. I was lucky enough to sit in the middle of the canoe and did not have to paddle. I took many pictures and was aware of the strong current that the guide and Brian had to fight as they paddled across.


We got to Brian’s truck at the AT parking lot in Caratunk and I practiced what woud become routine on my coming hike, the resupply stop. I refueled my Coleman gasoline stove, which I was starting to realize would not work for a long distance trip. I also got my fresh supply of expensive dehydrated food. Once the resupply was done, Brian and I decided to go out to dinner. We drove 10 miles to a general store in the Forks to get some snacks. We stopped on the way back to eat at a place called Three Rivers Whitewater (which I had been to on another rafting trip in the past) for dinner.


It happened to be all you can eat taco night as well as $1 draft night of 22oz Budweiser. We had a couple of beers and feasted on a dinner of tacos and refried beans. Of course, I ate all of this way too fast and ended up with a case of indigestion. We got back on the trail at about 6:30pm. I immediately ended up belching up some of the tacos I had just eaten for dinner. During the couple of hours that we hiked after dinner, Brian gave me the name of Taco Al. That night we camped right next to a stream coming down from Pleasant Pond about 3 miles hike in from Brians truck.


It was strange to wake up in the morning to a hiker passing by your tent, but that is what happened this morning. We decided to forgo breakfast this morning until we stopped at the next shelter by Pleasant Pond. We got to this shelter and did not even stop before going straight to the pond. The shelter just didn’t interest us at that point.


We sat on the public beach at the town of Pleasant Pond at 10am and met a couple hiking southbound from Monson who told me about the trail ahead while Brian finished carving his new hiking stick.


We relaxed and ate our food at the beach and climbed Pleasant Pond Mountain afterwards. This mountain had great views in all directions. The trip down from Pleasant Pond Mountain to Moxie Pond was the worst maintained section of trail that we had come across. The three mile descent had lots of blowdowns and was a very rough section of trail. At Joe’s Hole (a section of Moxie Pond that the AT goes near), we ran into the same lady that had passed by our tents at 7am. She told us that there was little water at the shelter that we were to stay at that night. We weren’t looking forward to getting there.


We passed a beaver pond where the AT was flooded out and rerouted over the top of the beaver dam that day. We got to the Bald Mountain shelter which had a dried up stream bed near it. The conditions had been so dry that the normally giant stream had dried up to a small trickle. However, a kind thru hiker had left Kool Aid drink pouches inside the shelter for hikers that were passing through, so Brian and I did enjoy a few drinks before going to bed (or so we thought).


As we were cleaning up from dinner, the first kid of about 10 arrived at the shelter. This group was a group of Portland city kids (preteens) who were totally unprepared for their hike. Their group had been hiking for three days at that point and were very tired, hungry and out of water. I ended up helping one of the counselors, by refilling 5 of their water bottles from the puddles of water that Brian and I had found after exploring the shelter area for a little while. The majority of their group had arrived at the shelter, but left a slower kid behind with another counselor. Luckily, they camped at the tentsite which was a quarter mile from the shelter, so we didn’t have to cram the 12 people into a small shelter.


We figured that their group was alright with the two mile downhill hike remaining to Moxie Pond so we continued our climb up Moxie Bald mountain. On our hike up, we saw where the group had left some water bottles the night before and also quite a few clothes left for a slow child at the same site. It was strange to wave to a logging truck at 8am, as we crossed a logging road, that was located right behind the shelter.


We got to the top of the Moxie Bald mountain, which I thought was similar to my first mountain of Mount Monadnock, we were surprised to see the remains of a recent forest fire. The fire had happened so recently that hoses were still spread out over the mountain. We found a spot were the fire was still smoking. We thought we caught a glimpse of Katahdin (but it ended up being Whitecap) in the distance.


We descended the last mountain of our trip and stopped for lunch at Moxie Bald Pond shelter. We read up on the adventures of our other fellow thru hikers that we had met before like Turkey Bacon and met up with an older woman northbound thru hiker that we would spend the next night with, Bluevest.


We left the Moxie Bald Pond shelter and the rain started to pick up as we hiked along. We had to cross two more minor streams and go through some marshes before we got to our next (and last) shelter at Horseshoe Canyon. This shelter was located next to a beautiful canyon that the Piscataquis River had carved through the valley. We finished dinner and the rain stopped. I had a chance to take a walk to explore the area and found a nice place to wash up in the river below the canyon.


This was our last day of my warmup hike on the trail and we were in a hurry to get to our destination, Monson. We left the shelter at 8am and forded the West Branch of the Piscatiquis River at 10am. We walked through the woods for another hour, left the AT on a side path and got to the paved road going to Monson. We walked the 2 miles to town by a nice slate quarry back to my car.


My long drive of 285 miles back home started at 11:30am, when we got back to Brian’s truck in Caratunk. We stopped in my old college town of Waterville for pizza, and went back in Portland to spend the night with our friend who started the hike with us, Cranbo. It was interesting to experience something that I would get to know quite well later on my hike. Everyone stayed away from Brian and I because of our smell as we waited in a Portland bar for Cranbo to get home from work, before we could hit his shower.


A week after this trip ended, I hiked a portion of the AT about 30 miles from Monson (at Gulf Hagas) and saw TurkeyBacon’s entry from the week before about finally being tired after almost 2100 miles. He was debating with other members of this shelter on whether Coke or Pepsi is better. The shelter concluded that Pepsi is for younger people and Coke is for older, more mature people.



Knife Edge, fall 2002


A month later, at end of September, Cranbo and I did a trip to hike the Knife Edge of Katahdin. We camped at the Katahdin Stream Campground and read about Turkey Bacon’s and Papa Geezer’s relief to be finished. Cranbo and I also hiked the AT southward from Daicey Pond, the night before climbing the Knife Edge, from Daicey Pond Campground to Big Niagra Falls. Very scenic! I also met another thru hiker, True, a girl from Maine who had been hiking the past season with people such as Turkey Bacon.



Final preparations


My mind was not quite completely made up that I would thru hike the following spring after this 60 mile backpacking warmup. There were only five months until I finished my certificate at Northeastern, but I was 90% sure that I would go. At my EMS job for the next six months, I thought of what I would need for my hike every day. I also tried and tested all the different gear in our store on a daily basis. I slowly bought new gear, so as not to spend all of my money at once.


I spent the rest of that winter doing weekend hiking trips to the White Mountains. This prepared me for any type of temperature and weather condition that I thought could come up on my thru hike. I did an AMC day hike to the top of Mount Lafayette in zero degree weather with ice pellets blasting me on the ridge. I spent a cold winter overnight at unheated Lonesome Lake Hut, introducing a couple of friends, Gary and Yinga, to the world of winter hiking.


Gary and Yinga standing on a frozen Lonesome Lake


The best preparation before my trip was to backpack with Brian up to Mount Chocorua. We stayed overnight at Jim Liberty cabin on the side of the mountain. Chocorua was not what I considered a hard mountain (under 4000 feet), but this overnight provided me with a great chance to prepare for the worst possible night that I thought I could experience on the Applachian Trail. It was about 20 degrees out that night.


The cabin had no front door to keep heat in. It started snowing outside that evening and the cabin was shared with a few people. I had the proper winter equipment along with a nice few swigs of Jagermeister and Brian and I kept ourselves warm all night long. We hiked up to the icy summit in snowshoes and crampons the next morning.

I worked with two former thru hikers at EMS, Coty and Junebug, who finished hiking the AT in 2001 and 2002, respectively. They gave me tips which provided invaluable to me. One example was that Coty had to talk me into buying hiking poles. To this point in my hiking life, I had only used poles to keep my balance when I was snowshoeing during the winter. I did not see the point of buying poles for hiking use in the spring or summer. Coty said that it would really save my knees on all the downhills. I am glad I listened to him! The other former thru hiker friend, Junebug, was just getting used to life back in society. She showed me all of her hiking pictures and told me some of her amusing trail stories.


One of my coworkers at EMS, Erika, was planning her own thruhike starting one week before mine. She and I both were very busy preparing that winter for our upcoming hikes by buying every little thing that we might need at our biannual employee discount sale. I had been at EMS for almost two years, so I had already bought almost every piece of hiking equipment I could imagine. I used this last sale to stock up on everything that I thought I would need for my maildrops. Smaller items like mountain soap, bug dope, snacks, and a few dehydrated dinners. I would end up using some of this stuff, but not all of it.


The winter of 2003 was spent finishing up my certificate program at Northeastern University in Burlington by taking three two hour night classes in one semester. I worked a full time job at EMS and added six hours of classes per week and had no social life. By January, I knew that I would be hitting the trail as soon as this semester was over. I had something to look forward to!


In the final few weeks, I drew up a schedule consisting of 15 mile days for the entire trip using a 2002 AT handbook that I had gotten for free at work. Using this schedule, I came up with a plan of shelters, maildrops and town stays for the entire trip. Knowing that this would be a very flexible schedule, I planned quite a few rest days into my schedule. I also planned on having visits from my family and friends at certain points of the trail.




















AT going away party


My final temporary farewell to Boston was a nice going away / birthday party at one of my favorite restaurants in Boston attended by some of hiking friends. It was a great way to say goodbye to all of my hiking friends for a year.


I got on the plane to Atlanta with my high school friend Gary. He came down with me for a long weekend in the city and to drop me at the beginning of the trail. I knew I was in for quite an adventure!


The long weekend in Atlanta was especially good for me. I got to tour the city before my hike which I had never seen before. I got to see the house where Mather Luther King grew up, the CNN Center and the Georgia state house. I also got to celebrate my birthday in the Buckhead section of Atlanta with a longtime friend before my journey began. Before I left to go to have dinner in the Buckhead area, I was supposed to meet a potential thru hiking partner, Soren from Copenhagen, Denmark at a local hostel.


I met Soren for a minute before leaving for the rest of the evening. Gary and I went across the street to a Krispy Kreme Donuts after meeting Soren, where I had my first Krispy Kreme donut ever. (a big national donut store chain that we did not have in New England)


The temperature change between Atlanta and Boston in late March was quite a big one. It was nice to go from daily 50 degree weather to daily 70 degree weather. It was also amusing for me to see that freeze warnings were issued for the north Georgia mountains where I was about to go. I had prepared for my hike in mostly sub freezing weather for the entire winter and this warning made me laugh.


The day after our city tour, we stopped at a park along the Chatahoochee River and made a quick visit to Gary's cousins in Alpharetta, who ended up easing my mind by taking me to the local outfitter to get some gas canisters for my stove (which were prohibited from airplanes and mail), and his cousins cooked dinner for us.


We got to Amicalola State Park in our rental car just after dark on March 30th. It took us some time to find the signs for the Amicalola Falls Lodge that we were staying at, but we eventually found the sign. We went up to our second floor room and fell right asleep. I am glad that my stove was shipped to this lodge and arrived in time for my hike so I would not have to eat cold food every night.

This list was a list that started in 2004, as part of an AMC camping trip to the Loj campground in the Adirondacks for July fourth weekend. Mari, Andy, Priscilla and I and went out to the Adirondacks for the first time to experience a new mountain range. The group hiked Mount Marcy together. Mari and I decided to break off from the group and go downhill via longer trail. I wanted to see where the Hudson River started. It was the year after my thruhike and I was still in good shape to hike a much longer distance than the rest of the group as was Mari.


It took me 13 years to set foot in the Adirondacks again. I had the urge to finish my NE111, which included all of the Adirondack 4000 mountains. 46 mountains are on this list and the mountains are much harder than the mountains of New England. I finished my final 46er mountains, Phelps, as the Covid pandemic was ending. It was not as crowded as usual in the Adirondacks on July 4

weekend 2020 with the Canadian border closed.


Pemi Loop and NH Grid


I decided to hike the Pemi Loop on the longest day of the year in June 2022. This loop includes 9 4000ers, 5 of which I needed for my June grid. Caroline joined me on this overnight hike. We did the hike as an overnight at Garfield shelter. It had been quite some time since I had backpacked. I enjoyed digging out my old AT backpacking stuff. We camped overnight across the street from Lincoln Woods trailhead to get an early start.


Caroline and Annelise, were 20 years younger than me, but I did manage to keep up with them for the Franconia Ridge. We hiked the 5 mountains of Franconia ridge. They pulled ahead at the Lafayette Garfield col. I started to slow down and they kept going. We met at Garfield campsite, a place where had passed the spur trail numerous times. It was nice to discover one of the few places in the Whites that I had never seen. The next day was another day that I sent them ahead after we hiked to Bondcliff together. I enjoyed the solo hike down from Bondcliff by myself. We met up on the Lincoln Brook trail below Bondcliff and did the long flat walk to Lincoln Woods together.

The New Hampshire White Mountain grid was my next list to conquer. This required much dedication. This list contains 576 mountains of the NH 48 4000 footers, 12 months of the year. Two weekends a month are spent in the White Mountains. I spent all my hiking time time in 2021 to 2024 on this. This list had been on my mind as I finished my NE115. I had 211/576 or 36% complete as of 2020, so it would be natural to start this list.


Hiking the same mountain again and again seems very repetitive, but hiking the same peak in different months via different routes is interesting. One can also see if you can beat your time when you do mountains in consecutive months. The White mountain hikes that I used to not like, Isolation and Owls Head, and the peaks with long road walks in winter, like Garfield and Carrigain, do not bother me anymore. There are a few people after the same goal like other lists I have done, but these hikers are usually local to NH. I pass the same gridders time after time. I mix these hikes in with some AMC trips and enjoy some of my hikes with bigger groups.


I finished 71 peaks for 316 in 2021. That October, I gridded out my first two peaks, Moosilauke in the morning and Tecumseh in the afternoon. This was a great feeling not to have to climb these mountains again. It was also nice to do the hikes in one day trip from Boston as opposed to having to stay overnight in the White Mountains. My most interesting hike that year was hiking up Lafayette and Lincoln and down the Lincoln slide to Owls Head. Caroline and I hiked up to Garfield at midnight and finished our hike at 2:30am. We both slept in our cars, but I had to meet Mari and Phil the next day for a hike over Crawford, Resolution and Stairs. As I only had four hours of sleep, I only made it to Mount Crawford before I had to turn around. I slept in Maris car as they completed their hike.


I finished 87 peaks for 403 in 2022. My best grid hiking day of 2022 in June, was doing the Wildcats and Carters one day and Zbonds the next for a nine peak weekend. I slept in my AT backpacking tent near the Zealand parking lot.


I finished 64 peaks for 467 in 2023. This was the first year of the NH grid that I finished in early June on Hale. As my summer months were gridded out, I decided to hike a new list, the NH52 with a view, until my grid peaks started again in October.


I started the 52withaview having completed 30 of the mountains. It was an easy list to finish. Peaks such as Tremont and Percy Peaks, brought me to places that I had never been to before but were within sight of peaks that I knew. (Carrigain and Cabot). I spent the summer of 2023 finish these mountains where I could hike two per day without a problem.


My first fall grid hike of 2023 was a hike on Jefferson and Adams where I rehiked a trail that I hated the first time I did it in 1985, the Link. The trail went over many rocks and roots and was not a great shortcut back to my car at the Jefferson Notch trailhead.


I finished 62 peaks for 529/576 (91%) in 2024. My most memorable September hike was a grid finish, Jefferson, for another hiker that I had hiked with before, Chase, and number 500 for me, Mount Adams. I did these with a fellow former thru hiker, Sean, who had never experienced a few hundred people at a random summit in the White Mountains. I had another hiker friend of mine, Michael, finish his grid on Mount Jefferson in 2016. A professional photographer took a nice profile picture of me on Mount Adams.


I finished the grid season of 2025 at 535/576 (93%) as I gridded out Whiteface and Passaconway in April. I had a long wait until Adams in November. My extended 2025 grid break was fun. I was able to help Phil finish off his hiking NE67 hiking list in the state of Maine. Mari, Phil and I enjoyed hiking Katahdin Hamlin from Chimney Pond, a way that I had not done when I finished my NE67 in 2004. Of course, it was cloudy so we didnt have a view as I did last time.

Mari finished her NE100 list with Phil, Andy and I on Pico peak near Killington. The most memorable hike of the year for Mari and I was Snow Cupsuptic where I had finished my NE100 in 2013. Mari, Phil and I found that one of the roads to the trailhead was blocked, so we could only go in and out from one side, the Route 27 side. She decided to leave it in Farmington and come home the next day, so I drove from home the next morning to get her.
